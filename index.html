About

Fair Questions

What venue owners
ask me before booking.

"Isn't opera too highbrow for our guests?"

Nobody needs to know the repertoire or the language. A live voice at full power, a few metres away, lands on its own. The guests who've never set foot in an opera house are the ones filming, and the ones asking when I'm back.

"We don't have a stage."

Neither do most of the places I sing. Dining rooms, terraces, courtyards. A voice and a room is the whole format. I bring the equipment, and setup takes minutes, not hours.

"We tried live music once. It just faded into the background."

Background music is built to be ignored. This is the opposite. When the first aria starts, conversations end on their own, and the room turns. It becomes the moment of the evening, not the soundtrack behind it.

"Our regulars come for calm evenings."

So we plan for that. We build the set together: when I sing, for how long, how the evening breathes between pieces. It's a hosted moment inside your evening, not an interruption of it.

"Isn't a full opera voice too loud for a dining room?"

The same training that fills a concert hall without a microphone also lets me sing beside a table without drowning a single conversation. Volume is technique. I use that control all evening, and it's exactly what a speaker on a stand can't do.
