About

I'm a principal singer at Theater Trier and a graduate of the Universität Mozarteum Salzburg, with fifteen years on professional stages. But when you book Opera Lux, the credentials aren't the point. You get me, in your room, singing for your guests.

I plan the set with you personally, song by song, around your venue and your evening. On the night I read the crowd: when to stay soft while people talk, when to let the full voice out. Every note is live. Songs people actually know.

Fair Questions

What venue owners
ask me before booking.

"Isn't opera too highbrow for our guests?"

Nobody needs to know the repertoire or the language. I sing at full power a few metres from the tables, and that lands on its own. The guests who've never set foot in an opera house are the ones filming, and the ones asking when I'm back.

"We don't have a stage."

Neither do most of the places I sing. Dining rooms, terraces, courtyards. One singer and a room is the whole format. I bring the equipment, and setup takes minutes, not hours.

"We tried live music once. It just faded into the background."

Background music is designed to be ignored, so it was. This is a planned performance with a start, an arc, and a finish, built with you around your evening. Guests stop mid-conversation because something is actually happening in the room, not because anyone asked them to.

"Our regulars come for calm evenings."

So we plan for that. We build the set together: when I sing, for how long, how the evening breathes between pieces. It's a hosted moment inside your evening, not an interruption of it.

"Isn't a full opera voice too loud for a dining room?"

I've spent fifteen years learning to fill a concert hall without a microphone, and to sing beside a table without drowning a single conversation. I adjust that all evening, room by room, set by set. A speaker on a stand can't.
